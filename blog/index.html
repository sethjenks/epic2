Big Data, Better…Offsite

Tuesday, November 6, 2012
by Julia

This is the first in a series of blog posts in which we talk about how we do things at Ginger.io, and what we think we do well.

This past week, Ginger.io East and West gathered for our first full team meet up in a long while. We spent a day debating customer priorities among the vineyards and a day continuing those discussions in San Francisco. At the end of the week, we were all refreshed and ready to push forward.

Given that we spent a lot of time as a team thinking about how to make the most of our time, we wanted to share what worked for us – and what we will be improving for our next all-hands meetings.

Do…

…divy up responsibility: We assigned each team member a topic and a discussion to facilitate. Some of these topics matched to functional role (e.g., sales lead Andrea led the sales update) while some asked people to leave their comfort zone (e.g., engineer Mahesh drove a conversation around hierarchy). Asking people to stretch into a facilitation role and to think about things that may not come up day-to-day helped enrich the conversation and make it more representative of everyone on the team.

…involve outside voices: One of the highlights of the offsite was having the chance to speak with a number of our investors and advisors, some in person. While this took quite a bit of scheduling gymnastics on the behalf of Anmol (and our investors’ assistants), we relished the chance to ask questions and learn from others’ experience. And we also enjoyed the friendly competition as each speaker tried to provide more pithy insight than the caller who spoke before!

…have a plan for follow up: The offsite left us with buzzing minds and an appetite for continuing iteration. Alas, we weren’t as prepared structurally for how we would continue that momentum. Luckily, we were able to flex our Friday calendars for some impromptu brainstorming that helped close the loop on many of our open issues. Since then, we’ve resolved to schedule brainstorming time into every week to close on the remaining topics – and keep the type of dialogue we started going.

Don’t

…expect to get through everything: It won’t happen. See the point above re: follow up.

…end the day at 5 PM: We spent a lot of time during the offsite talking about culture and what it meant to be a team that invested in each other as people, not just as colleagues. Some of the best moments of the two days came after work, over amazing salsa at Papelote or over drinks at a bar (surrounded by other start-up teams, of course). Those memories will be the ones we look back on – and use to roast each other at the Holiday party.

…forget to celebrate milestones: Props to Anmol for this one, who decided last minute that everyone needed a commemorative souvenir. We went with a T-shirt designed around an inside joke, and made sure to give one to everyone who participated and helped our new release. Now, when someone in the office pulls it out of a dresser drawer after a late night, it’ll evoke another late night when we all pulled together.

What tips do you have from your own offsites? What would you recommend we do to make next time even better?

Ginger.io featured by McKinsey at Health Datapalooza

Wednesday, October 3, 2012
by Julia

With the recent announcement that the 2013 Health Datapalooza will be June 3-4, we wanted to reflect back on last June.

We were very excited to be highlighted by McKinsey at the Health Datapalooza this past June. McKinsey Directors David Knott and Steve Van Kuiken gave a fascinating and polished presentation on Big Data in healthcare that highlighted Ginger.io as a company “shaking up traditional approaches to health data.” They’ve just recently passed the document on to us, and we wanted to share it as a “must-read” for anyone interested in health data.

We were particularly struck by a few of the insights – as recovering consultants ourselves, here are three:

  • Applying early successes at scale could reduce US healthcare costs by $300-450 billion. We are often asked what the market size is for something like Ginger.io. To put a number like this in perspective, this is 11-17% of the $2.6 trillion the US spent on healthcare in 2010.
  • Patient data should be a “natural resource.” While the idea of data as a commodity or currency is often floated in discussions like this, the natural resource metaphor feels particularly apt. Patient data is a well that must be tapped carefully to avoid externalities, with proper regulation and security.
  • There are opportunities both for innovators to disrupt, and for existing companies to step up and drive change themselves. For innovators, it is a question of defining the problem and scaling the solution. For established players, it is embracing the transparency and uncertainty that comes with being a leader in a brave new world.

What do you think? Which insights resonated most with you? Tweet to @ginger_io and let us know!

Mobile Health, a Key Partner in Health Care Reform

Monday, September 10, 2012
by Dr. Barnett

The United States spends vastly more money than any other country in the world on health care, yet we still lag behind on key health indicators like life expectancy, access to health care, or meeting standard clinical guidelines. The Affordable Care Act is an attempt to correct this, but it is a daunting aim for one bill to achieve. One of the biggest problems facing those attempting health care reform is the complexity of how to form solutions to epidemic overspending and poor quality. It’s not obvious at all how to fit the pieces together, at least until reading the lucid and readable perspective of Harvey Fineberg, president of the Institute of Medicine, in a recent issue of the New England Journal of Medicine.

Dr. Fineberg points out that health information technology (IT) is a crucial part of “the solution” to making health reform work:

“… The burgeoning availability of data provide a foundation for new functionality in health IT, which is already beginning to show promise as a way of improving the efficiency and effectiveness of care. When this functionality is combined with other advances in such areas as high-speed network connectivity, geospatial positioning capacity, wireless communication, robotics and artificial intelligence, biosensor technology, bioinformatics, and ingenious applications, one can envision an array of interrelated and interconnected uses of health IT …”

He lists several “Potential Uses of Health IT” which should sound familiar to anyone in the mobile health community:

  • personal medical records
  • personalized health reminders and follow-up
  • personal health, diet and activity monitoring and motivation
  • monitoring and advising of patients with chronic disease
  • enhanced peer-to-peer and professional-patient support

As an advocate for the potential of mobile health, I was very excited to read these priorities coming from the president of the Institute of Medicine, an incredibly influential force in health care. Dr. Fineberg recognizes that mobile health, at its best, bridges the enormous gap of care that happens between doctor’s visits and hospital admissions. I’d like to take his points a step further, and illustrate the ways in which mobile technology can deliver some of the “potential uses” that he outlines:

1) Personal medical records:

Mobile personal health records which can interface with the electronic health systems used by physicians could improve consistency of care by enabling patients to always have an accurate copy of their medical record wherever they travel. As the failed Google Health project taught us, you can’t expect any but the most motivated consumers to proactively track their own health data. But there are mountains of health data recorded every second that could be easily coordinated with software owned by the patient. Since we are unlikely to ever have a national health IT system, the next best option could be to have every patient in the US own their entire, up-to-date medical record.

2) Improving chronic illness data collection:

The US is becoming a nation of chronic illness: in 2005, 133 million Americans had at least one chronic illness. The top players in chronic illness include morbid obesity, diabetes, asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and congestive heart failure. All of these illnesses require active participation by patients to reduce complications from the illness. Diabetics must check and record their blood sugars, asthma and COPD patients monitor peak flows, and heart failure patients track their weight. Mobile apps can be a key solution for reinforcing healthy daily routines through intelligent reminders which can personalized for each user. It’s amazing for me to think that as a physician, I often have a visiting nurse assigned to my more complex chronic illness patients who help record this data and check in with patients, when this can clearly be done effectively with a single smartphone with the right software. This would enable visiting nurses and other caregivers to focus on care delivery and interpreting clinical data rather than measuring it.

3) Creating new passive tracking tools:

There is also the frontier of using a combination of passive and/or active tracking to follow patient’s progress and response to new trials of medication (this is the approach that Ginger.io takes). As I mentioned, there is a huge gap between the time when patients intersect with health care providers and the rest of their lives, when everything else happens. As health providers, we can often be deluded into thinking that our influence persists between infrequent visits. In most cases, nothing horrible happens if someone doesn’t floss very often or forgets to refill their statin prescription one month. But for patients with chronic illness, one or more months of lapsing on their medications or self-care can have disastrous and expensive consequences: emergency room visits, hospital admissions, multiple provider visits to re-establish control of the chronic condition. We can improve on this system with more intelligent tracking that patients and physicians both participate in to catch decompensation early.

How to be (and create) a Better Intern(ship)

Tuesday, August 28, 2012
by Julia

Mishal was Ginger.io’s summer engineering intern (you can read more about his story here). Because Ginger.io is all about transparency, we asked him to make his exit interview public. Below is his advice to other interns – and to us – about how to shape a memorable, valuable and fun summer experience:

What startups should do:

  • Make the intern gain ownership of his or her project
  • Let the intern get used to the codebase.
  • 3-4 weeks into the internship, have an actual talk about the direction of the internship. At this point, question them: do you know what you’re doing? Karan asked me this question and it made me focus on getting the dashboard to be a “pulse of the community.”

What interns should do:

Find Mentors

  • Have a specific mentor, someone who is willing to help you. Web Engineer Jimmy was AWESOME! I learned a lot.
  • Take a different member of the team out to lunch whenever you can.

Always Ask

  • Make sure you know why you’re doing what you’re doing.
  • Ask people for help. People might look busy and you might not want to interfere, but in the end asking is the way to go. This was hard for me to learn as I usually tried to not interfere in other’s work, but it was really important.
  • Ask questions, as it’s the way to learn. “An internship is about learning,” is what I’ve heard. And I think that’s true. I’ve learned very much about software development this summer.

Take Initiative

  • When you’ve nothing to do, find something to do. Ask anyone if you can help them with their current task. I had to do this once and asked Sabih, our designer. I was able to help with getting the mobile version of our blog to render properly on mobile devices.

An Intern’s Story

Thursday, August 23, 2012
by Julia

Ginger.io and I were paired through the Startup Summer program, which matched my interests in web programming and healthcare with Ginger.io’s needs. These past two months, I worked on a project involving data manipulation and visualization. Being at Ginger.io gave me practical engineering experience, as well as a chance to learn about working as part of a team and helping a growing company tell its story.

One Friday evening, Anmol, Jimmy and I went out to dinner and to check out an ice cream shop near Dolores Park. I used that time to question them about a career as a software programmer. While originally trying to get a feel for one career path, I learned a larger life lesson: everyone’s life has a theme. It’s your ability to identify that theme and create a compelling story around it that makes you appeal to others and allows them to join in your aspiration.

One example is our own story here at Ginger.io, where we are creating a product that allows for a more accurate picture of what happens for a patient between doctor visits. I’ve heard many times that more than the idea, it’s the people that make a startup successful. It’s true. When a cohesive team rallies behind a single story, it’s possible to bring that dream to fruition.

Interestingly enough, writing good code is like writing a story. As a web programming intern, I was exposed to production code, which makes me question the maintainability of my code as I am writing. Halfway through my internship, I read a quote that went something along the lines of “a collaborative programming project should look as if it has been written by one person.” This made me change my outlook of coding from getting a function to produce the expected output, to writing a function that’s maintainable as well. Because as an intern, I’ll not always be here to guide people through my code.

Having worked on the same project over the course of the summer, I realized that though I may have written some code, it doesn’t mean that I will be familiar with it several months from now. A well placed comment and appropriate variable names can go a long way in terms of code readability.

I will miss the team after returning to school for my Senior year at Harvard. I am looking forward to the learning and fun that happens during the semester, but I’m also looking forward to the two weeks before the semester starts when I can spend time with family and reflect on the lessons that I’ve learned about both personal and software development.

-Mishal Rahman
